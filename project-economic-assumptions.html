Economic Assumptions Automation

This case study documents the automation of economic assumptions frameworks, covering both inflation and long-term return modelling.

The objective was not to predict markets, but to ensure that assumptions used across analytics and reporting were consistent, governed, and reproducible.

Context & Problem

Inflation and return assumptions influence nearly every investment output — from performance expectations to scenario analysis and client communication.

Historically, these assumptions were maintained manually, often across spreadsheets, with limited version control and inconsistent application across analyses.

Design Objectives

Consistency

Ensure the same assumptions are applied across all analytics and reporting outputs.

Auditability

Enable clear tracing of assumptions used at any point in time.

Flexibility

Support scenario analysis without manual rework.

Framework Design

Assumptions were modelled as parameter-driven inputs rather than hard-coded values, allowing controlled updates without breaking downstream analytics.

Separate models were maintained for inflation projections and long-term return expectations, with shared governance controls.

Versioning ensured historical analyses could be reproduced exactly as they were generated.

Assumptions Coverage

Inflation Modelling

Structured inflation assumptions supporting real-return calculations and long-term projections.

Return Expectations

Asset-class level return assumptions used across portfolio analytics and reporting.

Scenario Analysis

Ability to assess alternative assumption sets under different economic conditions.

Impact & Outcomes

Automation improved governance of assumptions, reduced inconsistency across analytics outputs, and enabled faster scenario analysis.

It also significantly lowered the operational and reputational risk associated with undocumented or misapplied assumptions.

Key Takeaways

Economic assumptions are one of the most underappreciated sources of risk in analytics systems. Treating them as governed infrastructure rather than ad-hoc inputs is critical at scale.
